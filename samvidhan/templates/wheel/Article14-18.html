Right to Equality

Fundamental Rights of the Indian Constitution

Article 14: Equality before Law

Article 14 ensures equality before the law for all persons in India, including citizens, foreigners, and legal entities. However, exceptions include immunity for the President and Governors under Article 361, protection for publishers of true parliamentary reports under Article 361-A, and immunity for members of Parliament and State Legislatures under Articles 105 and 194. Foreign sovereigns, ambassadors, and diplomats also enjoy immunity from legal proceedings.

Article 15: Prohibition of Discrimination

Article 15 prohibits discrimination against any citizen on the grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. However, exceptions allow the State to make special provisions for the upliftment of women, children, and socially or educationally backward classes, such as reservations and access to free education.

Article 16: Equality of Opportunity in Public Employment

Article 16 ensures equality of opportunity for all citizens in public employment or appointments. However, exceptions allow reservations for backward classes that are underrepresented in state services. Additionally, individuals holding positions in religious or denominational institutions may be required to belong to the specific religion or denomination.

Article 17: Abolition of Untouchability

Article 17 abolishes untouchability and prohibits its practice in any form, making it a punishable offense. Those convicted of practicing untouchability are disqualified from elections to Parliament or state legislatures. Offenses include preaching untouchability, preventing access to public places, refusing admission to hospitals or educational institutions, justifying untouchability on any grounds, and insulting scheduled caste members based on untouchability.

Article 18: Abolition of Titles

Article 18 abolishes titles, barring the State from conferring titles on citizens or foreigners, except for military or academic distinctions. It prohibits Indian citizens from accepting titles from foreign states. Foreigners holding offices under the Indian State need the President's consent to accept titles from other countries. Additionally, no one in India can accept presents, emoluments, or offices from foreign states without the President's approval.

Case Example: State of West Bengal v. Anwar Ali Sarkar (1952)

In this landmark case, the Supreme Court of India ruled that Article 14 prohibits the State from arbitrarily classifying citizens for different treatment under the law. The Court struck down a law that allowed for special trial procedures for certain individuals, as it violated the principle of "equality before the law."
